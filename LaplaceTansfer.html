Struggle With Fourier and Laplace Transformation

Posted on 2019-05-27 | Edited on 2019-06-13 | In 正经的 , Learning Note | Comments: | Views:
As blogger's major is ME, and most of readers of this article will also be those who don't have enough background knowledge for the high level mathmatic, this note will emphsize on the application of the Fourier and Laplace transformation in the engineering fileds. I will also try my best to interpret those two complictaed therom with my personal understanding instead of tedious mathematical demonstration.

1. Fourier Series

1.1 Fourier Therom

Anyway, long long ago, a hero named Fourier saved the world from the ruling of complicated periodic function when he found that any function like that could be composed by a infinte series of sine and cosine function which was big deal even for the acient mathematicians. So the Fourier Therom could be represented as:

$$
\begin{equation}
f(x)=a_0+a_1cos(x)+a_2cos(2x)+ \cdots +a_ncos(nx) \\
\qquad \ \ \ \ +b_1sin(x)+b_2sin(2x)+ \cdots +b_nsin(nx) \label{eq01}
\end{equation}
$$
Where $f(x)$ is a periodic function whose $T=\frac{2\pi}{1}=2\pi$.

In the other form:
$$
\begin{equation}
f(x)=a_0+\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}a_ncos(nx)+b_nsin(nx)
\end{equation}
$$
The formal proof of the general Fourier Therom for all restricted but arbitary functions is carried by Dirichlet, and we call the conditions those functions in 1829, which could be extent to the Fourier Series, should satisfied Dirichlet Condition.

1.2 Fourier Coeffcients

What does $\ a_n \ $ and $\ b_n \ $ mean and how to define them?

We can take $a_n$ and $b_n$ as the amplitude of sine and cosine function with differnt frequencies, the higer amplitude, the more important role this specif function plays in the serie. And we will call them as Fourier Coeffcients

When we want to define them, we need to know some extraordinary propeties of those trigonometric function:
$$
\begin{equation}
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}cos(nx)sin(mx)dx=
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\frac{1}{2} \left[ sin(nx+mx)-sin(nx-mx) \right ]= 0 \label{eq02}
\end{equation}
$$
Equation \ref{eq02} uses Prosthaphaeresis Formulas and property of trigonometric function—intergration over a period is 0.
$$
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}cos(nx)cos(mx)=\left\{
\begin{aligned}
\ \pi & , m=n \\
0 & , m\neq n
\end{aligned}
\right.
$$
$$
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}sin(nx)sin(mx)=\left\{
\begin{aligned}
\ \pi & , m=n \\
0 & , m\neq n
\end{aligned}
\right.
$$

The mechanism of above two intergrations are same with what we use in equation \ref{eq02}.
Through those properties of trigonometric function we can get those Coeffcients.
$$
\begin{equation}
\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f(x)sin(mx)dx= b_m\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}sin^2(mx) \label{eq03}
\end{equation}
$$
As all the intergals, except which showed in the right side of equation \ref{eq03}, vanish, we can get:
$$
b_m=\frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f(x)sin(mx)dx\; ; \qquad m=1,2,3,\cdots \label{eqbn}
$$
In the same way we could get $a_n$:
$$
a_m=\frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f(x)cos(mx)dx\; ; \qquad m=1,2,3,\cdots \label{eqan}
$$
Noticeably, there are some differences when it comes to $a_0$:
$$
a_0=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f(x)dx
$$
Actually, it’s quiet interesting to note that $a_0$ is actually the average of $f(x)$ in the interval from $-\pi$ to $\pi$. I would like to call this constant as a basic wave for the whole series which decides where the following trigonometric functions will display their waves. More detail could be seen in the Sec 1.3.

1.3 Extension of interval

In most occasion, we need to face the signals and functions that have different periods, $2T$ for example, rather than have the specific period of $2\pi$. To enchant this weapon, we introduce the transformation variable:
$$
\begin{equation}
u=\frac{2\pi}{2T}t
\end{equation}
$$
The role $u$ played is to shrink or expand the period of specific functions as when $\pm x \to \pm T\; , \; \pm u \to \pm \pi$. So we have:
$$
\begin{equation}
a_n=\frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f\left(\frac{T}{\pi}u\right)cos(nu)du=\frac{1}{T}\int_{-T}^{T}f(t)cos\left(\frac{n\pi}{T}t\right)dt \\
b_n=\frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f\left(\frac{T}{\pi}u\right)sin(nu)du=\frac{1}{T}\int_{-T}^{T}f(t)sin\left(\frac{n\pi}{T}t\right)dt \\
a_0=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f\left(\frac{T}{\pi}u\right)du=\frac{1}{2T}\int_{-T}^{T}f(t)dt \label{eqextend}
\end{equation}
$$

1.4 A simple example

So far, through equation \ref{eqextend}, we could transformation any periodic functions into Fourier series. Now let’s take a suqare wave — one of the most common waveforms that we need handle for the signal processing, whose period is $T=4s$ and $Vpp=6V$. The waveform is shown in the following picture.




We can give the $f(t)$ in the section of $[-2,2]$ as:
$$
f(t)=\left\{
\begin{aligned}
-3 & , -2\leq t < 0\\
3 & , 0 \leq t \leq 2
\end{aligned}
\right.
$$
Using equation \ref{eqextend} we can get:
$$
\begin{equation}
a_n=\frac{1}{2}\left(\int_{-2}^{0}-3cos(\frac{n\pi}{2}t)dt+\int_{0}^{2}3cos(\frac{n\pi}{2}t)dt\right)=0 \quad n=1,2,3,\cdots\\
b_n=\frac{1}{2}\left(\int_{-2}^{0}-3sin(\frac{n\pi}{2}t)dt+\int_{0}^{2}3sin(\frac{n\pi}{2}t)dt\right)=\frac{12}{n\pi}\quad n=1,3,5,\cdots\\
a_0=\frac{1}{4}\left(\int_{-2}^{0}-3dt+\int_{0}^{2}3)dt\right)=0 \label{eqsquare}
\end{equation}
$$
Through equation \ref{eqsquare} we can rewrite $f(t)$ as:
$$
\begin{equation}
f(t)=\lim_{m\to \infty}\sum_{n=1}^{m}\frac{12}{(2n-1)\pi}sin\left(\frac{(2n-1)\pi}{T}t\right) \label{eq09}
\end{equation}
$$
The following picture shows differnt series when $\;m=2\;$,$\;m=5\;$,$\;m=10\;$, drawn in orange, blue and black respectively. And the following GIF could help us to relaize how the Fourier Series works more directly.






We could also represent $f(t)$ in another way which we called frequency spectrum like the following picture. In the frequency spectrum we can recogonize the importance of the waves with different frequencies.
We can find that with the growth of $\; m\;$, the series’s graphic will progress step by step to the real one. Now we have already got the weapon to fight with the periodic functions. But what can we do with aperiodic one. The answer is we need much powerful weapon, whose name is Fourier Transformation.


2. Fourier Transformation

2.1 Little tricks on Fourier Series

We find it’s cumbersome on both writing the equation and calculating the intergrations in form of trigonometric function. So we need do some little tricks on it.
First, we will replace $\; \frac{2\pi}{2T}$ with $\omega_1$. Next, by manipulating Euler’s Formula, more details of Euler Formula could be found in this article — Extremely Beautiful Formula --- Euler Formula :
$$
\\
e^{in\omega_1 t}=cos(n\omega_1t)+isin(n\omega_1t) \\
e^{-in\omega_1 t}=cos(n\omega_1t)-isin(n\omega_1t)
$$
we could get:
$$
\begin{equation}
cos(n\omega_1 t)=\frac{e^{in\omega_1 t}+e^{-in\omega_1 t}}{2} \\
sin(n\omega_1 t)=\frac{e^{in\omega_1 t}-e^{-in\omega_1 t}}{2i} \label{eqi}
\end{equation}
$$
Then substitute equation \ref{eqi} to Fourier Series, we can get:
$$
f(t)=a_0+\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\left( \frac{a_n-b_ni}{2}e^{in\omega_1 t} + \frac{a_n+b_ni}{2}e^{-in\omega_1 t} \right)
$$
Now let’s look back to our derivation of $\; a_n\;$ and $\; b_n\;$ in equation \ref{eqextend} we could find that $\; a_n\;$ is actually an odd function of n while $\; b_n\;$ is an even one. And we assume that:
$$F(n\omega_1)=\frac{a_n-b_ni}{2}$$
With above properties we can say that:
$$\frac{a_n+b_ni}{2}=F(-n\omega_1)
$$
If we assume $\; a_0=F(0)\;$, equation\ref{eqi} can be rewriten into:
$$
\begin{equation}
f(t)=\sum_{-\infty}^{\infty}F(n\omega_1)e^{in\omega_1t} \label{eq10}
\end{equation}
$$
And with the propery of exponential function we can get:
$$
\begin{equation}
F(n\omega_1)=\frac{1}{2T}\int_{-T}^{T}f(t)e^{-in\omega_1t}dt \label{eq11}
\end{equation}
$$
Now we have got the exponential representation of thr Fourier Series. I will call $\; F(n\omega_1)\;$ as spectrum coeffcient.

2.2 Strategy for the aperiodic functions

As we have mentioned before, the Fourier Series could only be used to represent some special (we will discuss in Sec3) periodic functions. The only thing we need to do to extend the application is to treat the aperiodic functions as a periodic one whose $\; T\to \infty\;$, which also means $\; \omega_1\to 0$.
What will happend with that? The frequency spectrum will be a continuous function instead of what we say in the above picture.
If we look back to equation \ref{eq11} we could find that $\;F(n\omega_1)\to 0\;$ which means this constant is useless or meaningless in this occasion. What we will do is to producting $2T$ on both side of equation\ref{eq11}:
$$
\begin{equation}
\frac{F(n\omega_1)}{\frac{1}{T}}=\int_{-\frac{T}{2}}^{\frac{T}{2}}f(t)e^{-in\omega_1t}dt \label{eq12}
\end{equation}
$$
I need to point that in equation\ref{eq12}, I assume period of $\; f(t)\;$ is $\; T\;$, instead of $\; 2T\;$ what we assumed before. And if we could realize that $\; \frac{1}{T}=k\;$, we could introduce a new coeffcient, the spectrum density coeffcient,$\;F(\omega)\;$, name of which could be interpret it is the average amplitude the unity of frequency. In this way we could get:
$$
\begin{equation}
F(\omega)=\mathscr{F}\left(f(t)\right)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f(t)e^{-i\omega t}dt \label{eq13}
\end{equation}
$$
Equation \ref{eq13} is the weapon that we could utilize to handle the aperiodic function and we call it as Fourier Transformation.
Correspondingly we give the inverse process of Fourier Transformation:
$$
f(t)=\mathscr{F}^{-1} \left( F(\omega)\right)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}F(\omega)e^{i\omega t}dt
$$

3. Laplace Transformation

3.1 The most powerful weapon

Even if we have g forge two extraordinary swords—Fourier Series and Fourier Transformation, we are still helpless when we are dealing with some functions that will not converge— not satisfied the Dirichlet Condition. While most signals we need to handle is depend on time, which won’t be less than 0, the only thing we need to do is add an negative exponent to $\; f(t)\;$ as:
$$
\begin{equation}
F(\omega)=\mathscr{L}\left(f(t) \right)=\int_{0}^{\infty}f(t)e^{-\sigma t}e^{-i\omega t}dt=\int_{0}^{\infty}f(t)e^{-st}dt \label{eqlaplace}
\end{equation}
$$
Where $\; s=\sigma+i\omega \;$, equation \ref{eqlaplace} is actually “Excalibur” forged by Laplace called Laplace Transformation. We can see the effect of $e^{\; -\sigma t\;}$, when $\; \sigma=0\;$, with the following GIF, and find out why we need to confine the interval from 0 to $\; \infty\;$.



3.2 Application of solving partial-differential equation

So far, we have already give the defination and the derivation of Laplace Transformation. Here is the question: What can we do with this weapon in the engineering area?

First, let’s see some excellent usage of Laplace Transformation:

$$
\begin{equation}
\mathscr{L}\left(\frac{df}{dt}\right)=f(t)e^{-st}\vert_{0}^{\infty} -(-s)\int_{0}^{\infty}e^{-st}f(t)dt=s\mathscr{L}\left(f(t)\right)-f(0) \label{eqf1}
\end{equation}
$$
With the same thought, we could also get:
$$
\begin{equation}
\mathscr{L}\left(\frac{d^2f}{dt^2}\right)=s^2\mathscr{L}\left(f(t)\right)-sf(0)-\frac{df}{dt}\vert_{t=0} \label{eqf2}
\end{equation}
$$
We should mention that, correspondingly, like what we do with the Fourier Transformation, we also have the inverse prosess of Laplace Transformation as:
$$
\begin{equation}
\mathscr{L}^{-1}\left(\mathscr{L}(f) \right)=f \label{eqinverse}
\end{equation}
$$
With equation \ref{eqf1} , \ref{eqf2} and \ref{eqinverse}, we could reduce the partial-differential equation to the total-differential equation.

3.3 An example of utilization of Laplace Transformation

We will take the heat conduction as the example to show the effect of Laplace transformation, in which we have differential equation of temperature distribution $T\left(x,\tau\right)$:
$$
\begin{equation}
\frac{\delta^{2}T}{\delta x^{2}}=\frac{1}{\alpha}\frac{\delta T}{\delta \tau} \label{eq17}
\end{equation}
$$
Where $\; x\;$ is the spatial variable and $\; \tau \;$ is variable in time domain. Applying Laplace Transformation on both side with respect to $\; \tau \;$:
$$
\begin{equation}
\int_{0}^{\infty}e^{-s\tau}\frac{\delta^2T}{\delta x^2}d\tau = \frac{1}{\alpha}\int_{0}^{\infty}e^{-s\tau}\frac{\delta T}{\delta \tau}d\tau \label{eq18}
\end{equation}
$$
Casues the intergration on the left sides are completely independent with variable $\; x\;$, we could rearrange equation \ref{eq18} as:
$$
\begin{equation}
\frac{\delta^2}{\delta x^2}\int_{0}^{\infty}e^{-s\tau}T(x,\tau)d\tau=\frac{1}{\alpha}\int_{0}^{\infty}e^{-s\tau}\frac{\delta T}{\tau}d\tau \label{eq19}
\end{equation}
$$
Now let’s we substitute equation \ref{eqf1} and \ref{eqf2} to equation \ref{eq19}, we can get:
$$
\begin{equation}
\frac{\delta^2 \mathscr{L}(T)}{\delta x^2}-\frac{s}{\alpha}\mathscr{L}(T)=\frac{T}{\alpha}\vert_{\tau=0} \label{eq20}
\end{equation}
$$

Through equation \ref{eq20}, we can say, we have rewrite the partial-differential equation with a total-differential one which are easy to prosess. The next and final thing we need to do is solving the experssion of $\; \mathscr{L}(T)\;$ and then use the inverse transformation to get $\; T(x,\tau)\;$.
